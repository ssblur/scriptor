# Writing Tomes

After discovering a few tomes, I decided to go to work trying to replicate the magic.
At first I was met with limited success, but I realized something: 
all the spellbooks I'd found around the world had gold inlaid into the binders.

This led to one of my first major discoveries: Spellbook Binders

<item href="scriptor:spellbook_binder"></item>

If I wrote a spell with a book and quill, I could craft up the written book with one of these to convert it
into a spellbook I could cast from directly.

But why not cut out the middleman? 
I figured if I could make a book and quill, and a spellbook binder, why not make a spellbook and quill?

This simplified my spellcrafting significantly, which meant I could get to the more interesting parts:

Spell Structure and Words

It seems that the world has a structure inherent to it that can't be changed.
This structure determines which order the words in a spell need to be in.

The first sort of component is what I'll call the subject, 
though that doesn't mean it's always at the start of every spell.
These determine how the spell is cast.
I've seen subjects that fired off projectiles, required the caster to touch the target, and targeted the
caster themself, though there may be more sorts out there.

The next type of word I've seen is what I've dubbed an "action".
These, I would hope intuitively, determine what a spell will do. 
I've seen spells with actions that set the target ablaze, heal them, or even create explosions!
There really seems to be no limit to what these things can do!

Lastly, and potentially most interestingly, are what I've been calling "descriptors".
These ones are weird, as they don't always have the same sort of effect, and not every spell even has one.
I mention one, because each spell can have any number of descriptors in a row, and they'll all modify it!

Descriptors can move where the spell targets, change the color of its projectiles and effects,
or do more devious things like hurt the caster, though if this is to an effect I'm not sure.

And regarding spells' structures, I've noticed that while every spell has only one subject,
some spells will have more than one action, and groups of descriptors with each action.
When I've cast these spells, every action has occurred, though with only the descriptors that were next to it.

These groups of actions and descriptors were in the same order as in the first part of the spell,
though without an additional subject.
There was a word I didn't see anywhere else between each of these "sections" of the spell. 
Maybe that's the key to including multiple actions in one spell?

There's certainly more to be known about writing spells, but I am still only just learning about this art.
Perhaps I'll ask one of the villagers?
I've seen one of them toting around what looked like a spellbook...
